## Origin

Roughly ten thousand years ago, somewhere in what is now central Asia, a man of an early human tribe ate a flower. The flower carried a virus the species had not encountered. The virus integrated into his germline and rewrote him — stabilised, because he had eaten the flower itself. He stopped dying. He saw things ahead of time. A field formed around his body that changed other bodies near him.

He fathered children before the change went too far. They inherited the gene from his germline alone — they never ate the flower. The gene took in them, but in an unstabilised form.

**He sealed the remaining flowers himself.** Of every bloom of that species he could find, he kept exactly one — sealed inside a crystal sphere no later tool has cracked — and destroyed the rest. He did not want any more like him to be made. The flower species died with that act. The one bloom inside the sphere is the only one left in the world.

Then he carved binding runes into a great standing stone himself and stepped inside their bounds. The field collapsed inside the carving. He has stood there ever since. The tribe died out within a generation. His diluted descendants scattered. The gene scattered with them.

He is still on the stone. The stone is in what is now Antarctica. He has not died in ten thousand years. The crystal sphere with the last flower travelled separately through human history.

## What the flower does

To eat the flower is to **stabilise** the gene. To carry the gene without ever eating the flower is to slip, eventually, into uncontrolled expression — mutation. The gene wants to keep writing. The flower is the only thing that tells it where to stop.

The protohuman sealed it because he understood both halves: the gene needs the flower to be safe, and the flower would make more of him. He chose extinction over inheritance.

## What the mist is

The mist is the **helix expression field in airborne form,** leaking from any unsealed piece of S-0's body. Three rules:

1. Where S-0's flesh is sealed — the Antarctic binding stone, the Building B cavern binding-circle replica when active — the field is contained.
2. Where it leaks — ruptured airborne-conversion containers, sample-storage breaches, cracks in containment, severed pieces sitting unsealed — the mist exists.
3. What the mist does to a body depends on whether that body carries the helix gene:
   - **Helix-positive body in mist:** full expression. The gene fires on every codon it was built for. The carrier wakes into their abilities.
   - **Helix-negative body in mist:** forced expression on a gene the body doesn't carry. The physiology tries to run a program it doesn't have the source code for. It breaks. That is the origin of every zombie, every hunter, every half-mutant in the campaign.

That is the entire physics. Every observable behaviour of the mist traces back to it.

## Mechanics for helix-positive PCs

- **Levels 1–5:** stable expression. Abilities behave as normal class features. The mist enhances and protects rather than threatens.
- **Level 6 onward:** random mutations begin to surface as the gene starts writing past its safe envelope. Each level-up after 5 carries a mutation event. In parallel, mist resistance climbs — the body adapts to its own field even as it slips.
- **Level 7 onward:** every use of a spell or class-derived ability carries a small chance to trigger an immediate mutation event.

The only stabiliser is the flower. Eating it stops the mutation track. There is exactly one bloom, sealed inside the Asterweiss sphere in the Director's Office of Building B. The sphere has never been cracked by any tool any carrier has tried.

The bloom cannot be shared across four people.

# Timeline

## The long quiet (10,000 years to ~1880)

The gene survives in diluted form, scattered across humanity. Most carriers live full lives without ever expressing — the conditions required to wake it are too rare. Occasionally one ends up near a strong enough trigger and goes strange: a saint who heals by touch, a seer whose dreams come true, a witch the village burns. The history of human "magic" is mostly mistakes about people accidentally expressing a gene nobody knew existed. None of these expressions ever stabilised — without the flower, every awakened carrier eventually slipped into mutation and died, was killed, or hid.

The standing stone in Antarctica stayed where it was. Ice covered it. No one looked for it because no one knew to.

## 1880s — Wittenbach and Silvertongue in the Pamir

**Zacharia Wittenbach.** Austrian ethnographer, born in the 1840s. Worked the Pamir corridor through the 1880s, recording oral traditions of isolated highland populations. The stories he wrote down described a man who could not die, sealed under a stone at the southern end of the world, and a flower he had taken with him.

He did not work alone. From **1885** his field partner was **B. R. Silvertongue** (b. 1865), a young Cambridge-trained philologist sent out by his college on a Mesopotamian-script grant that had bled sideways into proto-Indo-European fieldwork. Silvertongue spent six summers in the Pamir at Wittenbach's side. He was present in the cave alcove the morning Wittenbach found the four broken tablet pieces; he sketched them in situ; he sat with the elder who told them the oldest of the stories and rendered the philological core into Latin.

Wittenbach died of fever in 1891 before publishing. His Pamir guide, who had translated the oldest of the stories for the two men from the local dialect, was found drowned in a mountain stream two weeks later. Silvertongue, twenty-six, went home to Cambridge with his own field notebooks, the tablet drawings, and a working partial decoding nobody had asked him for. He carried the work for the next fifty-seven years.

## 1903 — Papers reach the Esterhazy library

Wittenbach's field notes and four bound notebooks pass through the Vienna academic underground until they reach the **Esterhazy** library in 1903. They sit in the Esterhazy private archive, occasionally noted by visiting scholars and never published, until a young American doctor seeks Esterhazy patronage in the early 1930s on the strength of a letter Marian Esterhazy receives describing symptoms she has read about exactly once before.

## 1908 — Marian Esterhazy born

**Marian Esterhazy** (1908–1975). Old central European patronage. Inherits the family library, including Wittenbach's notebooks, in her twenties. Will fund Halder's pre-1962 Antarctic expeditions. Her family seat passes through her descendants to a small body of patrons-of-record and government insiders that will, after WWII, formalise as the Committee.

## 1919 — Halder born

**Everett Halder** (1919–1984). American. Helix-positive from birth — the gene runs latent in his lineage. He grows up with small things he cannot explain: a sense for which patient will not survive the night, an ability to slow his own heart and breathing past anything taught at medical school, an untouchable quality at field hospitals during the war. He hides all of it. By his late twenties he has a private vocabulary for what he is and believes he is the only one.

In **1932**, age thirteen, he reads in a French medical journal a single-paragraph footnote referencing the Wittenbach papers and the Esterhazy archive. He files the reference away. In **1938**, halfway through his medical training, he writes Marian Esterhazy a letter describing his own symptoms in careful clinical language. She replies within ten days. By spring 1939 he has read the notebooks in Vienna twice and is on a ship south.

## 1939 — Halder's first cut

Halder reaches the stone on a private Antarctic expedition funded by Esterhazy money, the first Westerner to do so. The protohuman — designated **S-0** by Halder's own log — is bound exactly as Wittenbach's stories described. Conscious. Eyes tracking. Cannot move. Can speak only in a whisper too faint to make out at more than a foot's distance. Feels everything.

Halder takes **what he can justify to himself.** Skin from the back of the left hand. A small tooth from the upper jaw — the only one that comes out easily. A core sample from the right thigh, taken with a trocar designed for liver biopsies. Forty millilitres of fluid drawn from a vein in the neck.

He works for two hours. He does not speak to S-0. **S-0 does not stop watching him.** S-0 chooses not to whisper anything — he could have, and Halder would have heard him. He decides at some point in the first thirty minutes not to give Halder a single word. Halder feels the decision happen and does not understand it. He recovers the sealed crystal sphere with the last flower inside, leaves the runes intact, and goes home.

The seven men on the expedition who saw S-0 awake sign papers in the camp that night acknowledging they have been exposed to *experimental medical material* and consenting to lifelong medical observation. Two kill themselves within the decade. Two more develop unexplained chronic illnesses and die before 1960. The remaining three sign extended NDAs every five years and drink.

**The 1939 yield will be the only piece of S-0 anyone touches for the next forty-six years.** Halder rations the samples across the entire 1962–1984 research programme.

## 1945–1962 — Halder builds the institutions

Halder returns from the war with samples, the sphere, and the conviction that he has found the source of every old story about the supernatural in human history. He never eats the flower. He cannot crack the sphere. It will sit in his Building B office for the rest of his life, in plain sight, the only thing that could stabilise him locked inside a crystal he cannot open.

He spends seventeen years building the institutions that will let him work on it: a private research foundation, a Boston site, a small circle of vetted scientists bound by classified medical contracts, and a federal funding line that nobody on the Senate appropriations side ever sees. His private register of helix-positive carriers grows steadily through medical anomalies, war records, missionary reports, asylum logs, and the diplomatic-pouch correspondence of Esterhazy contacts across the old continent.

## 1962 — Founding

Halder formally launches **Project Chimera** as a classified US programme under quiet medical-intelligence contracting. Same year, he founds the **Halder Foundation** as its civilian face — a respectable philanthropy and research institute that gives Chimera a clean shell to operate behind.

The **Boston site — Building B — comes online the same year.** Halder designed it personally. Two purposes braided together in the architecture:

1. A **sheltered residence and clinic** for helix-positive individuals located through his register. The upper floors look like a quiet research institute. Wood-panelled rooms. A library. A small chapel. Garden access through a south-facing sun-room. Music allowed at any hour. Patient files written in present-tense narrative, not chart shorthand.
2. A **research programme** to characterise the gene, work the 1939 tissue samples, and search for any chemical or biological substitute for the flower's stabilising effect.

### The 1962 founding team

All hand-picked. All loyal to Halder personally.

- **Dr. Lenore Aspe** (1928–2003). Virologist, Hopkins-trained. Halder's clinical right hand. Runs the tissue-sample work and the patient programme on the residence side. Believes she is working on the answer to several unsolved questions in human pathology. Does not know the political layer above her. Trusts Halder absolutely.
- **Dr. Lionel West** (1922–1967). Oxford biochemist. Leads the flower-substitute synthesis effort. Brilliant, restless, ethically fierce. Closest of the founding team to Halder personally.
- **Col. Byron Archer** (1924–1979). US Army medical operations officer. Federal liaison and security architect of the programme. Honest soldier. Believes Chimera is a legitimately classified medical project of national importance.

Outside the immediate team: **Marian Esterhazy** in Vienna, holding the family seat on what is now formalising as **the Committee** — a small body of patrons-of-record and government insiders who oversee Chimera at the strategic level. Halder reports to it quarterly. The Halder family in Boston: Halder's son **J. Halder II** (1942–2018), then a young man, learns the cover-story version and never asks for more.

## 1965 — Aliz meets Tomáš

**Aliz Shane** (Hungarian, b. 1930, in Halder's register since 1959, unexpressed, played the cello) and **Tomáš Shane** (Czech, b. 1926, in the register since 1962, unexpressed, bookbinder by trade) meet inside Building B and marry with Halder's blessing. They are among the most stable long-term residents on the upper floor.

## 1967 — The Old Laboratory

This is the first thing that breaks Halder.

Lionel West, depressed and impatient with three years of failed syntheses, **succeeds** where nobody knew he had — he has produced a partial analogue of the flower compound from the 1939 yield, and he injects it into himself one night alone in the building. He leaves a note on his desk that reads only *Tell me if it works.* Halder finds him the next morning.

What Halder finds is not a corpse. It is a thing that had been West a few hours ago — bones partly displaced, skin partly remade, one eye gone, the other watching him with full recognition. West tries to speak. The sound it makes is not language. Halder stands in the doorway and cannot move.

West dies in the lab forty minutes later, after the partial expression burns out the heart it was rewriting. Halder seals the room himself. He destroys the synthesis notes, leaves the chalkboard from his own last working day on the wall, and never tells Aspe what he saw. The 1967 lab is not preserved as memorial. **It is a quarantine, dressed as a memorial.** The chalkboard sealed under glass is the lie laid over the door. Halder's last working day on it is dated three days before West's death.

From 1967 onward Halder runs the programme as a man trying to atone for an accident he caused by trusting a colleague.

## 1968 — Iris born

**Iris Shane**, born May 1968 inside Building B's residence clinic. Aliz and Tomáš's only child. Conceived knowing — or hoping — that a child of two helix-positives would inherit the gene from both directions and might express differently from anyone in the register. Aspe delivers her. Halder is in the room.

The double inheritance expresses at birth. Iris is born already mutated — and, unlike every other carrier in history without the flower, the mutation **stabilises on its own.** The gene, getting itself from both parents at once, writes her into something that holds.

She is a **high-elf mutation.** Pointed ears, paler than her parents, the wrong proportions for a newborn human in ways that do not resolve as she grows. Tall, slender, ageless. She will stop ageing visibly around twenty-three and never start again.

She is, as far as Halder will ever know, **the only successful stable expression in the history of the programme.** Halder tries to repeat it. Two other dual-positive pregnancies are carried in the residence during the 1970s. Both children are stillborn, mutated past viability in utero; the second is recognisable as a thing that was almost a child and is still in a stasis jar in the B3 cold vault. Halder stops authorising the practice after that. Iris remains unique.

**The Committee votes in November 1968 to take her at six months of age** for study under their custody. Halder personally overrules the vote by walking into the Committee's quarterly session with a draft press release outlining the entire programme in detail and threatening to leave for the *New York Times* offices that afternoon if the motion is not withdrawn. The motion is withdrawn. **The Committee never forgets.**

## 1971 — Gideon

**Gideon March** (b. 1947). Recruited by Halder out of a DoD procurement office. Not helix-positive, not a scientist — a competent administrator with a federal-contracts background and the kind of careful, neutral face that survives bureaucratic warfare. Halder begins grooming him as personal successor.

Gideon is sincere in this period. He believes in Halder's protective mission. He has no reason to think otherwise yet. He spends 1971–1980 reporting to Halder honestly.

## 1972 — The first non-voluntary resident

**Henrik Vogel**, age 51, asks to leave the upper floor. He has decided the slow-mutation prognosis is worse with treatment than without and wants to go home and die outside. **Halder approves keeping him under sedation against his consent.** Aspe administers. Vogel's wife is told he passed; the body is "cremated." He lives another six years in a locked room on the upper floor, sedated to docility, occasionally lucid enough to ask Aspe what day it is. He dies in 1978 of organ failure brought on by the sedation regimen itself.

This is the second thing that breaks Halder. He tells himself it is mercy. Aspe carries it without speaking.

## 1973 — Edmund born

**Edmund Halder** is born to J. Halder II and his wife Margaret. **Helix-positive from birth** — the gene runs latent in every Halder direct line. Halder logs his grandson's profile privately, hopes the kid lives a quiet life, and decides not to bring him into the programme.

## 1974 — The second non-voluntary resident; Geneva arrives

A second resident, **Cordula Mertz**, age 48, is kept against her will after she attempts to leave the campus. By 1977 there will be **five non-voluntary residents under "extended palliative protocol"** on the upper floors. Aspe administers each one. She has stopped objecting.

**Geneva Loomis** is hired the same year as **Iris's private tutor.** She is twenty-seven, well-credentialled, recommended through a quiet channel that traces — though no one in the building knows it — to the Committee. **She has been a Committee asset since 1971,** placed in three different positions to get her into Building B at the right moment in the right role. She will teach Iris languages, mathematics, history, and music for the next ten years. Iris will love her without reservation. Geneva will reciprocate, in the limited way her brief permits.

## 1975 — Marian Esterhazy dies; Klaus inherits

Marian Esterhazy dies. Her seat passes to her son **Klaus Esterhazy**, late 30s, careful, ambitious, contemptuous of his mother's old-money idealism. Two other Committee seats turn over in the same window: a US defence-procurement insider replaces a retiring academic, and a Swiss banking principal replaces a deceased one. **By 1978 the Committee has a working majority that no longer sees Chimera as a medical project.** They see it as a strategic capability that has been wasted on humanitarian sentiment for fifteen years.

## 1976 — Aliz and Tomáš slip

Both Iris's parents begin to express within months of each other — Aliz in February, Tomáš in May, the body's natural mutation window for long-stable carriers ageing without the flower. Aspe administers the protocol on both, gently, in the residence clinic where Iris had been born eight years earlier. Iris, age eight, is told they have been ill for a long time. She is already old enough to know it isn't the whole story and she does not press.

Halder formally adopts her the next month and gives her her parents' surname. Aliz and Tomáš's ashes are placed in an urn on the residence chapel's east wall. Geneva continues to tutor her.

The mask starts early. Halder teaches Iris to be quiet about her appearance from the time she can understand the words. She is kept off-campus carefully — not from shame but because Halder understands the Committee will not leave her alone if her existence becomes too visible. She reads the entire residence library by twelve. She is sharper than anyone around her by fourteen. Halder tells her to never show it. She listens.

## 1977 — Kallisto incorporated

J. Halder II becomes Foundation Board Chair. Same year, Gideon March pitches and executes a restructure: the Foundation's applied-research arm is spun out into a **private biotech corporation — Kallisto Biomedical.** Argument: a corporate structure can take federal contracts, operational risks, and bear public exposure the Foundation legally cannot. Halder approves it. He still trusts Gideon completely.

This is the moment, in retrospect, when the corruption becomes structurally possible. Kallisto answers to Halder personally, then to Gideon — not to the Foundation Board.

**Henrik Loew** (b. 1948), a quiet, principled biochemist out of Heidelberg, joins the antiviral programme as a junior researcher the same year. Halder marks him, privately, as a future trusted continuator.

The 1967 lab is formally re-plaqued as "memorial" with the Kallisto restructure — the quarantine is dressed in fresh paint and the building's official tour now stops at the chalkboard under glass. West's body has been in a sealed cold drawer in that lab for ten years and remains there to the present day.

## 1978 — Sokol; Vogt arrives

The Committee, through Klaus, requests via Gideon a single experiment: a controlled forced-expression event on a willing carrier, to characterise the helix's awakened state for the strategic register. Halder refuses.

**The experiment happens anyway.**

Subject: **Reuben Sokol**, age 44, a stable resident of the upper floor for nine years, never expressed. **Dr. Hannes Vogt** — by then six months into the building under his paediatric immunology cover, brought in on Gideon's recommendation — does the work in a converted B2 holding room over the course of a single weekend in November 1978 while Halder is in Washington testifying before a closed Senate committee.

Sokol survives the expression. He does not survive his own awakened state. By Sunday morning he is something six men cannot subdue. They lock the holding room and weld the door. They keep him alive in there with hosed-in water and meat through a slot Vogt cuts in the wall the following Tuesday. He stops being recognisably Reuben Sokol within the first eighteen months. By the third year he has grown too large to lie down; he sleeps standing. By the tenth year he produces a constant low subharmonic that vibrates the door frame and that the staff who maintain his cell have learned to feel in their teeth before they hear.

**He stays in that room for forty-eight years.** The building's oldest unwilling inhabitant. Three different generations of maintenance staff have hated the duty. Hadj fed him through the 80s. Her successor fed him through the 90s and 00s. A third attendant fed him until the rupture. The mist reaches him on April 14 and writes him further for three days. The strike vaporises him on April 17 — the only end available to him.

When Halder returns from Washington he is told the experiment was cancelled. He believes it for three years.

## 1979 — Archer is killed; the paediatric trial is compromised

**Col. Byron Archer** dies in August 1979. Officially a heart attack at home in Bethesda. In fact, the cardiologist who had been adjusting his medications for three months — **Dr. Mira Hadj**, paid through a Foundation grant line nobody audits — has been running a separate, darker line of work in the building. Hadj has been quietly euthanising upper-floor residents who slip, on a faster timeline than Aspe would have approved, to free clinical hours for the Committee's experiments. **By her retirement in 1989 she will have killed eleven residents whose deaths Aspe attributed to natural progression.** Archer was an outside extension of that practice. He died because he had begun asking Halder questions about Vogt that Halder couldn't yet answer.

Hadj continues in the building's quiet medical staff until 1989. Aspe never knows.

Same year, Halder runs a **small protective trial on identified helix-positive children** — eight kids, including six-year-old **Edmund**. The trial is documented as a paediatric immunology study and Halder's intent is purely observational.

**Behind Halder's protocol, Vogt runs a parallel exposure track** using diluted aerosolised samples from the 1939 yield — testing for forced juvenile expression at small doses.

**Three of the eight children die** during the trial — **Sara Almeida** (age 5), **Markus Iversen** (age 7), and **Aliyah Demir** (age 6). Their families are told the cause was viral encephalitis. The bodies are cremated by Hadj. Two more children sustain quiet neurological damage and are followed up for years under cover of unrelated specialist care, growing up into adults who do not know what was done to them.

Edmund Halder is one of the three who comes out clean — Halder watches Edmund's blood panels personally and pulls him from the trial early when he notices Vogt scheduling extra sessions. Halder never asks Vogt why. The hidden dossier on the trial survives. The names of the eight children are still in a Kallisto file under Vogt's old initials.

## 1980 — Gideon is briefed

Sometime in 1980, Klaus Esterhazy meets Gideon March privately in a Foundation safe house outside Boston. The conversation lasts six hours.

Gideon walks out the man who allowed Sokol to happen. He had not known about the experiment in 1978. He is told that night. The recording of the experiment is played for him — eleven minutes of audio from the welded holding room, ending in a sound that is not quite a man and not quite an animal. He sits through it without changing his expression and at the end says: *"We'll need a separate medical line for cases like that."*

Gideon is from that day on the Committee's man. He still loves Halder. He still believes, in the cleanest private corner of himself, that he is the only person who can navigate what is coming. He also believes, in the corners next to it, that the residence floors will eventually be the price.

## 1981–1983 — Vogt expands; Halder begins to learn

Vogt's work in the building escalates under Gideon's quiet cooperation. Documents that should reach Halder reach him a week late. Foundation funds that should flow to the synthesis line are redirected. Aspe notices the budgets shifting; she raises it once with Halder, Halder is too tired to fight, the matter is dropped.

In late 1982, Aspe finds a discrepancy in the cold-storage inventory of the 1979 trial samples — there are more vials than there should be, in handwriting she does not recognise. She brings it to Halder. He spends eight months pulling threads quietly. By summer 1983 he understands:

- The 1978 Sokol experiment happened despite his refusal. Sokol is somewhere in the building still alive. He goes down to B2 alone one night in October 1983 and stands outside the welded door listening for ten minutes. He does not open it. He goes back upstairs and is quietly ill in his office bathroom.
- The 1979 paediatric trial was compromised and his own grandson was inside it.
- Archer's death was not natural.
- Hadj is doing something he does not yet understand on the residence floors.
- Someone close to him has been letting this happen for years.

He cannot prove who. He suspects Gideon. He cannot accept it.

He begins drafting the **exposure document.** Forty-three pages. Names Vogt, Hadj, the Sokol experiment, the three dead children, the budget redirections of 1980, the cardiologist's role in Archer's death. He keeps it in his office safe.

He hides the **Loew letter** in the plinth chamber of a statue on the residence side — naming Henrik Loew as the trusted continuator of his original work. Aspe is told the letter exists. She is not told what it says.

## March 1984 — Halder is killed

By February 1984 the Committee knows the draft exists. They cannot let it leave the office.

Klaus Esterhazy's preference is for **the building to be complicit in itself** — for the act to happen in such a way that no one in the institution can later pretend they did not know. The compound is a slow neurotoxin Vogt developed earlier in the year specifically for this purpose: it mimics cerebral haemorrhage on every test and takes thirty to forty-five minutes to act. Klaus selected the formulation. Geneva will deliver it.

The compound is delivered through Halder's evening tea on a Thursday in late March. The tea is brought by **Geneva Loomis** — Iris's tutor for the past ten years, the Committee's quiet on-site agent for the past thirteen.

Halder takes the cup and recognises that Geneva is not looking at him the way she normally does. He understands what is in the cup before he drinks it. He drinks it anyway — because by then he has decided that he wants the draft to survive him and the act of being killed in his own office is the thing most likely to make Aspe ask the right question afterward. He is wrong. The draft does not survive him.

He takes forty minutes to die.

**Iris is in the office the whole time.** She is fifteen. She came in through the connecting door from the study at 7:30 to ask Halder about a maths assignment. She saw Geneva enter and saw the way Halder's hand stilled when he took the cup. She slipped behind the bookshelves on the east wall and watched her adoptive father die from a gap between two folio editions of Vesalius. Geneva sat in the visitor's chair the whole time. Geneva and Halder did not speak. When Halder's breathing finally stopped Geneva took the cup, washed it in the office sink, dried it on her sleeve, and left.

**Geneva saw Iris on her way out.** Their eyes met for two seconds in the doorway. **Geneva let her live.** She has never told anyone why. Iris has never known what happened to her after 1995.

**Gideon** comes into the office at 9:15. He finds Halder. He finds Iris still hidden behind the bookshelves, hyperventilating, eyes blown out. He does not call for help. He sits down in the visitor's chair and tells her, quietly, that her father had a stroke, that she needs to go to bed, and that nothing she thinks she saw was real. She lets him say it.

By Friday morning Gideon has burned the draft in his own apartment, presented the stroke story to the Foundation board, and accepted the position. Aspe never knows. Iris speaks at the funeral. She thanks Gideon for his guidance. She is sixteen.

She has been wearing the mask he gave her that night for forty-two years.

## 1985 — First return expedition authorised

By autumn 1985 the Committee has authorised the first return expedition to the stone. Gideon signs the order. The team is not a Halder team — it is a Kallisto operational team built around Hadj and two of Vogt's protégés. They are briefed that S-0 has been "in continuous observation" since 1939 — which is true in a narrow sense — and that the expedition is medical rather than extractive — which is not.

## January 1986 — The harvest

The team reaches the stone. S-0 is exactly as Halder left him in 1939. **He still watches.** He still does not speak. They take far more than Halder did.

- The remaining teeth of the upper jaw (six).
- The right eye.
- A cross-section of the left forearm — the whole forearm, severed at the elbow with a bone saw over the course of an hour, packed in stasis and shipped intact.
- Approximately two kilograms of musculature from the chest wall, taken in strips.
- Bone marrow extracted from the femur through a heavy bore.
- Three litres of cerebrospinal fluid.

S-0 is awake for all of it. He does not die. He cannot. The runes hold his vital functions inside the carving. His tissue regenerates. New skin, new bone, new flesh fill in over the cuts in weeks. The new tissue is never quite the same shape or texture, but the same DNA.

One of the medical staff on the 1986 expedition kills herself on the journey home.

## May 1986 — Yield arrives in Boston

The first substantial pieces of S-0's body arrive on US soil. The left forearm is the centrepiece — pristine, alive, immobile, in a sealed B3 cold vault that did not exist under Halder. The vault was built in the year after Halder's death, in anticipation of an expedition the Committee had already authorised in principle while he was still alive.

## 1987 — Register digitised

Halder's old paper register of helix-positive carriers is digitised under Gideon's authority. The original cards survive in a Foundation archive vault; the working register from this point onward is electronic, searchable, and quietly auditable by anyone with Committee-level clearance. The children of the 1979 paediatric trial are tracked into adulthood under cover of unrelated specialist follow-up.

## 1989 — Residence wind-down begins; Hadj retires

New admissions to the upper-floor residence stop in 1989. The carriers who live there continue to be cared for — by Aspe officially, by Hadj's quiet line in practice — but no new helix-positives identified through the register are brought to Boston after this year. The protective programme is being closed without anyone announcing it.

**Mira Hadj** retires the same year, age 67, after twelve years of practice in the building and eleven kills she will carry to her grave. She moves to a small house outside Lisbon and dies in 2003 of natural causes. Her notebooks are buried with her at her instruction.

## 1989, 1993, 1997 — Subsequent expeditions

Each takes more. **1993 expedition takes the right hand**, severed at the wrist by Vogt's senior protégé, **Dr. Lukas Brandt**, who has by then moved up to lead the Antarctic medical team. The hand will be the anchor Vance later uses for her binding-circle replica in the B3 cavern.

By the late 90s the cadence settles into a **quarterly rhythm** of smaller takes from a permanent on-site team.

## 1990 — Permanent Antarctic base

The 1989 expedition leaves a wintering crew behind. By 1990 Kallisto has built a permanent Antarctic facility within reach of the stone — buried, low-profile, supported by the same shipping line that handles the cargo back to Boston. Headcount fluctuates between twelve and twenty staff. The base has its own medical team, its own security, its own coded comms to Boston.

## 1991 — Casetti and the sphere

Between January and March of 1991, a sixty-three-year-old Italian materials scientist named **Dr. Bianca Casetti** is brought to Boston as an external consultant under Halder Foundation grant *"non-destructive characterisation of historic crystalline specimens."* Casetti is a senior researcher at the University of Naples Federico II, specialising in the materials-archaeology overlap. ### Note on Bernard's great-grandfather and the Pamir line

**Dr. B. R. Silvertongue** (1865–1948). Cambridge-trained philologist of Mesopotamian and proto-Indo-European scripts. Wittenbach's field partner in the Pamir from 1885 through 1891 (see the 1880s entry). After Wittenbach's death he returned to Cambridge, took a college fellowship, and spent the next five decades quietly working through the tablet drawings and field transcriptions. His translations and interpretive sketches were never published — he died in 1948 with the work in manuscript form.

His papers and the one physical fragment he kept in family hands (Fragment III in the four-fragment set; see `tablet_fragments.md`) passed to his son, **Bernard's maternal grandfather**, who held them in the family for the rest of the century. The grandfather was the one Hartwell befriended, the one who hosted Todd in 1989, and the one who quietly passed Fragment III to Todd that afternoon (see the 1998–2024 Todd thread). After the grandfather's death the archive descended through the maternal line and now sits in a small private library at the old Cambridge house, held by a relative on Bernard's mother's side. Bernard has the right of access through the family but has never used it.

**The papers are not in any university collection.** Silvertongue refused institutional capture across his life; his son honoured that. MIT was never offered them.

**One outside researcher has accessed the archive: a junior Kallisto archivist named A. Yates, in 2014.** Yates presented himself to the relative holding the archive under an academic cover (Kallisto-funded Mesopotamian-script research), was granted reading rights for several days, took notes, and left. The relative did not understand what they were stewarding. Yates left Kallisto four years later citing *conscience.* Yates knows what Silvertongue knew.

The Sumerian tablet the PCs took from the Main Library safe is one of a set — Silvertongue's working notes describe **five tablets in the historical group, of which HF-A-1 broke into four pieces in antiquity,** each fragment carrying a partial coordinate set that, assembled, points to a precise location in the **Pamir corridor in eastern Tajikistan,** near the head of the Fedchenko glacier. ## Why the strike happened (DM — the real motive)

What Iris tells the PCs is true at the surface: federal command authorised a tactical strike because the cordon could not contain the event. - **The Committee wanted it gone anyway.** Kessler's reports read the harvest as a **successful-but-incomplete trial:** activation and control proven, **stabilisation (the flower) still missing, the mutation field a brand-new uncontrolled hazard.** They had the *data.* What remained on-site — the tanks, the program, the dead, the paper trail — was pure liability, with federal investigators three days from the basements. - **The three days (14th→17th) are the Committee's window:** assess Kessler's reports, write the site off, shape both the strike and the cover story, and burn it before the investigation reaches anything that matters.

**The blind spot that drives the rest of the campaign:** the Committee burned the site believing its lost assets died in it. They didn't — the four were already in the tunnel. # Aftermath — the first 72 hours after the strike

The PCs are in the tunnel. Outside the tunnel, the world is responding in real time.

## Detection breaks the cover instantly

A tactical nuclear detonation cannot be hidden, even at sub-kiloton yield.

- **Seismic.** USGS detects it within 30 seconds. Independent stations in Iceland, Sweden, and Japan correlate the signature within 4 minutes. The waveform is unambiguously a fission event.
- **Light flash.** Civilian aircraft north and west of Boston see it. Passenger footage is on Twitter in two minutes.
- **EMP.** Local power flickers across eastern Massachusetts; some unhardened electronics fail.
- **Satellites.** Russian, Chinese, EU, and Israeli imaging satellites flag the heat signature within minutes; analysts on it within twenty.
- **CTBTO** (international nuclear-test monitoring) registers the event and notifies 184 member states within the hour.

The "controlled demolition / chemical accident" cover dies in the first 90 minutes. The administration's initial *classified DoE criticality* story buys a single news cycle and then collapses.

## Hour 1–24 — panic, scapegoat, first containment

- Boston metro is in mass evacuation. Logan halts. New England civilian airspace closes.
- The White House goes dark for forty minutes. POTUS to a continuity-of-government site.
- Wall Street halts trading; Asia opens crashing.
- Russia and China demand explanations through diplomatic channels.

By hour 8 the public story is *classified DoE criticality.* By hour 30 the Committee has crystallised the longer-term cover: **Kallisto did unauthorised research and caused a criticality event.** Kallisto is sacrificed wholesale. Several Kallisto board members including **Dr. Vivian Crale** are detained. The Halder Foundation goes into legal lockdown.

## Chimera in the aftermath

**Project Chimera never enters any public-facing story.** It remains classified above the threshold where investigators can ask on the record. The Committee weaponises that classification.

- **The wall around the investigation.** Every federal investigator hits Chimera-classified files within 48 hours and is blocked or routed through Committee-aligned overseers. The trail that should run Kallisto → Chimera → Committee instead chases Kallisto board members who know nothing.
- **Loew and Alice Peters** are held under Chimera protocols — material-witness framing, classified interrogation panels, location closely held. Loew gives careful answers and mentions none of the load-bearing things. Alice has not been told Sean is dead; she suspects.
- **Sean's overt-compartment Director chair** is filled within 48 hours by **Dr. Adam Strauss**, a long-time Chimera oversight seat-holder and Gideon's quiet ally. Strauss runs Chimera from a Pentagon office under federal escort.
- **Chimera's covert compartment is operationally paralysed.** Boston is glass. The DC-area annex cannot operate without Boston inputs. The cold vault material is buried. The antiviral programme is iced. The Frankfurt route is frozen — Vance (if alive) is the only person with the contact protocols.
- **Edmund Halder** has the Halder Foundation Board Chair and standing to demand answers. The Committee cannot quietly remove him. He becomes, inside week one, the single most dangerous person to the Committee outside the four PCs.

## What the public believes by hour 72

- ~40% accept the official line.
- ~30% are convinced this was a foreign attack covered up.
- ~20% are convinced this was a domestic bioweapon programme the government had to destroy.
- ~10% in conspiracy-coded territory: aliens, secret weapons, occult.

Reddit, 4chan, Twitter, Discord have at least three viable open-source threads piecing together Halder's Antarctic expeditions, Kallisto's biotech registrations, the 2013 entrance closure, and unusual Foundation grant allocations. The internet is much faster than the federal investigation because the internet does not need clearance.
