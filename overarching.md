# The campaign choice — navigation, not dialogue

The campaign's final decision is not a dialogue option. It's a navigational one. By the time the PCs have to choose what to do next on the map, they're choosing between two endings without necessarily knowing it.

- **Pamir first** → S-0's summons pulls them into the time loop → self-consistent loop ending → Chapter 1 below.
- **Antarctica first** → if they destroy present-day S-0, the summons-anchor is severed and the loop never happens → world-altering ending → Chapter 2 below.

We design Chapter 1 fully now. Chapter 2 is a stub; we build it only if the table navigates that way.

### The summons-anchor mechanic (shared underpinning)

Pre-binding S-0 reached forward across ten thousand years and pulled the four PCs back to him. He could do this because his unbound expression bent causality at the source-scale (same trick Iris does small, at the seer-tier; S-0 is the maximum case). The summons is held open by his continued existence in the present — present-day Antarctic S-0 is the anchor.

- This explains how the bubble works (no separate pocket plane; the PCs are pulled, not entering)
- This explains why they can never go back (the anchor is in chains once he binds; later, in chapter 2, severable by destroying the anchor)
- This explains why he reached for *these four specifically* (he saw forward; saw the binding succeed; saw the Killer carrying his pattern; reached for the people who would make it possible)

### The geographic trigger

The summons fires when the PCs cross the threshold where past-S-0's reach is anchored — the place in the Pamir from which he reached. In the present, the spot is empty highland, maybe ruins, maybe a single weathered stone marker. The PCs walk across it expecting nothing. The empty valley becomes a living village around them. They were always going to cross it because S-0 always summoned them there.

When they're spat back to the present after the binding, they exit at the cave (the destination, not the threshold) and the bubble seals. Looking back at the village's location is empty ground again.

# Chapter 1 — The loop ending (Pamir first)

## Concept

The PCs arrive in the Pamir. They cross the threshold. The summons pulls them ten thousand years back. They spend their first phase in the village — meeting NPCs, learning what's about to happen, watching the two factions argue about it. Then they travel to the cave. On the way, or in the cave, the tablet breaks into the same four pieces they've been hunting all campaign. In the cave they participate in S-0's binding. He teleports south to the Antarctic stone. The bubble collapses. They emerge into the present at the cave entrance.

The horror is *recognition*, not responsibility. Trying to kill S-0 in the cave fails, or doesn't take, or kills the would-be killer. The only path through is the one that produces the canon they came from. Free will at this scale is an illusion.

## The load-bearing causes

The things the PCs *do* in the past that make present canon canon.

- **The Killer is why S-0 agrees to be bound.** S-0 sees his own future in the Killer's face — the man who carries his pattern — and chooses the cage. (Front-runner for the cave moment.)
- **Sally is the binding mechanism.** Her Channel Divinity — the leash she's been using on the Killer all campaign — is the suppression-pattern the binding stone is shaped to. The tribe's elder identifies her by feel. Without her there is no binding. *Brandt's B3 circle in 2024 is a recreation of Sally's work, ten thousand years late.*
- **The PCs are the anchor for the teleport south.** S-0 cannot cross the world alone; they anchor him to the Antarctic stone. (Still tentative — confirm.)
- **The tablet breaks during the journey or chase.** Whatever breaks it in the past is the cause of Wittenbach finding four pieces in the 1880s.

## The binding-side pitch (what an elder says to the PCs)

> "Lú nu-úš has been holding himself back since before our grandmothers' grandmothers. He has stopped holding. When he opens fully, every one of his kin in the world — every man and woman who carries his pattern in their blood — will open with him, in the same instant, wherever they stand. We have seen one slip. The slip never stops. He has seeded ten thousand generations. When he goes, they all go. The land we cannot see goes with them. The binding does not kill him. We do not kill him. We bind him so the kin inside the world do not all open on the same morning."

This is the rupture written at planet scale, in tribal mouths. It is the same threat Iris was holding off her whole life. It is true.

The elder identifies Sally separately, by the feel of her pattern: **"the one who holds the leash — we feel her in the air. She is the seal. The binding stone is shaped to her work. Without her we have no binding."**

## The village — three named NPCs, plus S-0

Village population ~30–40. Two factions in tension: a **binding side** (consensus, S-0 must be sealed) and a **proto-Listener side** (S-0 must not be sealed — his expression is sacrament, the binding is sin). Yates's spiritual ancestors, ten thousand years early.

Three named villagers only. Everyone else is background.

1. **The map-bearer.** First face the PCs meet. Carries the tablet. Reads warm. Recognises the strangers as the figures in the binding-side prophecy. Sets the tone of the whole village. *(Not yet sketched.)*

2. **The Listener.** The antagonist. Single person, not a leader + a thief — same body does both the warm village conversation and the mountain pursuit. The deliberate inverse of the Killer: same gene-expression toolkit, treated as sacrament rather than curse. Outwardly human throughout. Mechanically borrows from 5e monsters to make a level-10 encounter actually interesting:
   - **Read Thoughts** (Doppelganger). In the village this is the real horror of the conversation — finishes the PCs' sentences, knows their arguments before they make them, says something to the Killer only the Killer knows. In the chase, evades because they know where the PCs are going.
   - **Spider Climb.** Makes the vertical pursuit work mechanically. They run up rock faces the PCs have to burn resources to follow.
   - *(Third ability deliberately not specified — the tablet-break will emerge from whatever happens at the table, not from a trigger ability.)*

3. **The binding-side elder.** The figure who explains what the binding is for, identifies Sally as the seal, delivers the pitch above. Functions as exposition for the cave scene so S-0 isn't forced to monologue. *(Not yet sketched.)*

Plus **S-0** himself, pre-binding, ambulatory, conscious. Handled separately when we get there.

## How the tablet breaks

No DM trigger. The chase climaxes **at or just inside the cave** — that's where it has to end, because canon says Wittenbach found all four pieces in the alcove in 1885. So whatever the table does, the chase resolves at the cave.

Two natural paths to the trigger:

- **Failed Perception during a long rest on the journey.** The Listener steals it. PCs pursue. The pursuit ends at the cave (the Listener was always heading there for ritual reasons of their own, or got cornered there). The fight/chase resolution produces the break.
- **The chase happens inside the cave proper.** The Listener catches the PCs at the alcove rather than fleeing. Combat in close quarters. Tablet ends up on the floor, broken.

Either way, four pieces scatter around the alcove. Three settle loose on the floor (eventually become Fragments I, II, III via Wittenbach in 1885). One wedges deep into a band of binding-stone in the back wall and becomes Fragment IV — Wittenbach can't pry it free in 1885, leaves it there, where the PCs themselves can recover it post-loop.

## Wizards and casters

There are no wizards in the village. No D&D-style arcane casting in this world at all — HELIX runs on gene-expression. The village's "magic" is helix work: the binding stone, the suppression-pattern Sally carries, the Listener's outwardly-human expression-derived abilities. Anything that looks like a spell is being done by the gene.

## Open mechanics questions

- **Are the villagers aware of the PCs?** Or do they walk through them, see them only obliquely, recognise them as something prophesied?
- **What happens to the Killer at the cave moment?** Does S-0 acknowledge him directly? Touch him? Speak to him? What does the Killer experience that the others don't?
- **How does the binding ritual physically work?** Sally's CD is the seal — but what does she do? Stand on a marked spot? Channel through the tribe's ritualists? Lay hands on him?
- **What do the PCs hear / see at the moment S-0 teleports?** They're spat out to the present — but is there a sensory beat first?

## Two encounters — design checklist (for when we sketch them)

**Encounter 1 — Travel or village (lighter).** Sets up the Listener as a real threat before the cave fight. Could be: the Listener attempts to steal the tablet during a failed-Perception long rest and the PCs pursue, OR a skirmish with proto-Listener zealots on the climb to the cave. Either way: scaled to challenge but not threaten TPK. Establishes Read Thoughts in social context so the players know what they're up against.

**Encounter 2 — S-0's chamber (the cave fight).** The campaign-climax combat. Design checklist:

- **Real clock.** Binding must begin within 5-6 rounds or S-0 fully expresses and the world-end stakes the elder pitched actually trigger. No turtling, no long-rest reset.
- **Multiple components.** Listener (boss) + proto-Listener zealots arriving in waves. Action-economy parity with the party.
- **Legendary Actions for the Listener.** Standard 5e boss kit — 3 LAs per round between PC turns. Solves action economy against a single boss. Read Thoughts is a *flavor* ability: she narrates the PCs' impending choices, names their secrets, calls Sally's CD by feel. Creepy as hell, but no separate combat mechanic on top of the LA pool.
- **Sally is the priority target.** Listener knows she's the binding mechanism (Read Thoughts during the village stay). Party has to actively defend her.
- **Drop at least one PC to zero.** Calibrate so one body hits the floor during the fight. Doctor can pick them up; the visual is what makes "we nearly died" land for the 5e-savvy player.
- **Compromised reserves.** PCs enter the fight at 70-80% (rough village stay, climb hazards, no fresh long rest). Don't let them start at 100%.
- **S-0 is not the boss — he's the patient.** Cooperating, not hostile. His pre-binding expression leaks into environmental effects (causality-bending bursts, time-warps in 10-ft areas, gravity flickers). Hazard, not antagonist.
- **The binding ritual happens during the fight, not after.** Sally channels while the others defend. The fight ends when the binding completes (S-0 teleports south, bubble collapses) or when the clock runs out (everybody dies).

# Chapter 2 — The world-altering ending (Antarctica first)

**Stub. Build only if the table navigates this way.**

Sequence: PCs go to Antarctica before Pamir, locate present-day S-0 (bound under the stone), find a way to destroy him there. Destroying him severs the summons-anchor; the time loop never happens; the past becomes a path-not-taken.

Implications to design when needed:
- **What happens to the Killer**, who carries S-0's pattern. Dissolves? Stays as a normal man with no expression? Dies with him? This is the central player-stake of this branch.
- **What happens to the gene worldwide.** Existing carriers don't disappear (they're already alive), but does the gene fade over generations without a source? Or do all living carriers slip at once because the suppression-anchor is gone?
- **How they actually destroy him.** S-0 has been bound and alive ten thousand years; he is not trivially killable. The method has to be specific and earned.
- **What the world looks like in the aftermath.** Different ending tone — they prevented a future rather than recognising a past.

## Notes on what NOT to do

- **No deus ex machina time travel mechanism.** The pocket isn't a place; it's a summons. Fits HELIX's biology-and-gene physics.
- **No alternate timelines or branches inside the loop ending.** The loop is self-consistent.
- **No D&D magic systems imported into the village** — no wizards, no Shatter spell, no arcane stat blocks. Gene-expression only.
- **No DM trigger for the tablet-break.** Emergent from table action.
- **No two-NPC antagonist faction** (leader + thief). The Listener is one body doing both the village conversation and the mountain pursuit.
- **No exposition justifying the campaign choice to players.** They make the navigational decision; consequences are consistent; they don't need to be informed about which ending they're picking.

## The shared dream (PLAYED — Pamir foreshadowing planted)

Shown to the four PCs during their long rest at Wider's cabin. It foreshadows the binding scene in the cave: the cleric facing S-0 with hands open (the seal), the other three guarding outward, the bard speaking S-0's name, the tribe chanting worship. When they stand in the Pamir cave at level 10 — Sally channelling, the others holding the dark, Bernard's voice carrying the binding-word — *this* is what should click as recognition.

### What was read

> Stone. You are inside stone, somewhere you have never been, and you have already been here a while before you notice you have been here. Light comes from further in and you do not remember walking toward it.
>
> A figure is at the centre of the light. You cannot see him clearly. He is sitting, or kneeling, or standing; the dream does not commit. He is breathing — slow, patient, the sound of a thing already underway that you have walked in on partway through.
>
> Four sit around him in a tight circle. One — facing him, both hands open toward him, palms up. The other three with their backs to him, facing outward into the dark. They are not looking at him. They are watching what is around them.
>
> One of the four is you. The other three are the people sleeping beside you tonight. You can almost see through them.
>
> Further out, many more figures are gathered. You cannot count them. You cannot see their faces. They are facing the centre.
>
> The figures chant a strange phrase: "ilū šū".
>
> The bard, his back turned to the figure like the others, draws a breath and speaks a phrase "lu nu-us". Without choosing to, so do you. The same word, on the same breath. Two syllables. Heavier in your mouth than any word should be. It does not come from your throat; it comes from somewhere lower, somewhere older than breath.
>
> The figure at the centre lowers his head. The light goes out. You wake. The word is still on the back of your tongue and you cannot say it aloud.

### What the words mean (DM only)

- **"ilū šū"** — *"he is god."* What the gathered tribe chants: worship and dread of the figure at the centre (S-0).
- **"lu nu-us"** — S-0's name/title, what the bard speaks. The undying one. The PCs cannot translate either yet; the recognition lands in the Pamir.
