# Tablet HF-A-1 — the four fragments

The Sumerian tablet the PCs took from the Building B Main Library safe is **HF-A-1**, one of five tablets in the historical set Silvertongue worked on. HF-A-1 itself broke into four irregular pieces in antiquity — Wittenbach found it already in pieces in a cave alcove in the 1880s and shipped them out under separate cover. They have been apart ever since.

The party carries one piece. The other three sit in three different places, each tied to a different arc.

---

## Fragment I — Bottom-left (the party)

Already in play. Bernard carries it. Contains the western arm of the hexagonal map, the Hindu Kush edge, and a partial inscription line. Was in the Halder collection because Wittenbach's notebooks reached the Esterhazy archive in 1903 and Marian Esterhazy quietly traded a single piece to Halder's grandfather in the early 1960s as part of the founding gesture.

---

## Fragment II — Vienna (Esterhazy archive)

**Where:** in the Esterhazy library in Vienna, shelved beside Wittenbach's original 1880s field notebooks. Has been there since 1903. Klaus knows it exists. The Committee has never read it as load-bearing — to them it has always been an antiquarian footnote next to Wittenbach's far more useful oral-tradition notes.

**What's on it:** the centre of the hexagonal map. The target marker. The sexagesimal numerals.

**Why it matters:** without the centre piece, Bernard's bottom-left fragment is only an edge — directional, not located. Vienna closes the map.

### Clues the PCs can find pointing at Vienna

These are arranged from easy to hard, so the table can layer them depending on how much the players are pulling.

1. **Mansion archive (when they reach it).** A 1962 typed inventory of *Esterhazy library, on-loan and on-deposit Halder-relevant material,* compiled by Halder's grandfather, lists *"Pamir tablet fragments II–IV (Wittenbach 1885), Esterhazy archive Vienna, on-deposit, irrevocable."* The word *irrevocable* is underlined in pencil — Halder's hand. Beside it, a margin note: *"Marian will not release. M.E. holds remaining three."*

2. **Wittenbach excerpts in Silvertongue's working drafts** (Mansion archive or, harder, MIT). Silvertongue transcribed Wittenbach's field-log entry for the alcove discovery. The entry reads, in his careful Cambridge hand: *"Four pieces, broken anciently. Sketched in situ. Three shipped to V. via the Russian post; one retained for the drawing."* The *one retained* became fragment I; the *three shipped to V.* are the other three.

3. **Edmund's Foundation records.** A 1972 visit photograph — Halder's father, J. Halder II, in his thirties, on a courtesy visit to the Esterhazy library. Behind him, on a side table, a small clay piece in a felt-lined display tray. **Only one piece visible.** If the PCs run an image search through Edmund's research assistant, the photograph surfaces in a Foundation Board annual report from 1973.

4. **Yates knows but will not volunteer.** Yates accessed Silvertongue's sealed MIT papers in 2014; he read the same Wittenbach transcription Silvertongue copied. If the PCs reach Yates and ask plainly, he will confirm Vienna holds at least one — but he will also note something Halder's grandfather never wrote down: *"The Esterhazy register lists three. Only one has ever been seen on display. The other two, if they exist, are in the private vault."* This is the second-order beat — Klaus may know more than the public inventory suggests.

### Status of the other two "Vienna" fragments

Per canon-as-of-now: only **one** of the three Vienna-shipped pieces is the live fragment II in the archive. The other two — historically — never reached Vienna intact. Russian-post damage, customs intercept, a lost crate; the table can pick the cause when it matters. **Functionally there are only three live fragments left: II (Vienna), III (Todd), IV (Pamir).** Fragment I's edge + II's centre + III's inscription + IV's site reading assemble the full message.

If the table wants a fifth thread later — a missing or destroyed piece, recoverable only from rubbings or from Wittenbach's traced copies — the lost Vienna shipments are the hook.

## Fragment III — Todd has it (secret)

**Where:** in Prof. Aaron Todd's office at USP São Carlos, in a locked drawer under correspondence files. Wrapped in cotton inside a small wooden cigar box. He has had it since 1989.

**How he got it:** the tea visit in 1989 — already in canon (STORY.md §1998–2024 / the Todd thread). Hartwell brought Todd to Silvertongue's home one afternoon as a postgrad. Silvertongue, in the last months of his life and increasingly convinced his MIT bequest would be sealed beyond useful reach, made a quiet decision: he handed Todd a small wrapped object and asked him to *"keep it until someone in my family asks for it by the right name."* Todd does not know what the right name is. Silvertongue did not tell him. Hartwell may have known but is dead.

Todd kept the piece for thirty-five years without knowing exactly what it was. He suspected; he did not press. In late 2024, when Iris pre-briefed him through Hartwell's widow as an intermediary, Iris confirmed what the piece was and asked him to hold it until Bernard came for it. **Todd has been waiting for Bernard's call since.**

**What's on it:** the right side of the hexagonal map and the bulk of the proto-cuneiform inscription — the readable text. Without this, the map is a map without instructions.

**The reveal beat.** When Bernard contacts Todd through any normal channel and identifies himself as Silvertongue's grandson, Todd asks one question — something only the family would know (open at the table; pick from Bernard's existing player canon). On the right answer, Todd opens the drawer. This is the moment Iris designed for; it is not a clue chain, it is a recognition scene.

### Optional foreshadowing breadcrumbs (use sparingly)

- **In Silvertongue's MIT papers** (accessible only through the Hartwell channel — the widow has a key to one drawer her husband never told MIT about): a handwritten note dated November 1989 reading *"the small piece I let A. carry south."* "A." is Aaron Todd. Bernard can decode this if he reads his grandfather's correspondence carefully.
- **Hartwell's widow** can be drawn out to mention an old letter from Silvertongue to her husband from late 1989 that included a phrase about *"a piece in safe hands across the equator."*
- **Iris's prepared materials** (the dossier they may still be carrying or that Casetti may surface): a single line, *"Todd holds III. Identify yourself. He will know."*

None of the above is required. Bernard contacting Todd is the trigger; the breadcrumbs only exist for tables where Bernard's player would rather have earned the reveal.

## Fragment IV — The Pamir site

**Where:** at the Pamir corridor itself, near the head of the Fedchenko glacier, in the cave canon already places there (STORY.md §503 — the cave with the original tribe's wall inscriptions and the second sealed sphere). The fragment was never removed from the site. Wittenbach saw four pieces in the alcove and took only three out; the fourth was too deep into the wall, embedded in old binding-stone, and he could not pry it free without destroying the inscription around it. He left it. It is still there.

**What's on it:** the southern arm of the hexagonal map and the closing line of the inscription — the line that names what the other three pieces together direct the reader to find. The grandfather's polished translation closes with this line. Without IV in hand the reader can decode the *location* of the cave but not the *meaning* of what the cave holds.

**Clues:** none needed. The map itself, once assembled from I + II + III, points the party to the Pamir. They go because the map tells them to go. The fourth fragment is the reward for arriving, not a separate hunt.

# Why this distribution works

- **I (party)** is what they hold. The hook.
- **II (Vienna)** forces a Committee-adjacent leg. Klaus's town, his archive, his patronage relationships. The party has to either steal it, lean on Edmund's Foundation channels for a legitimate request, or use Yates's old route. Each opens different threads.
- **III (Todd)** rewards the relationship Bernard already has. It is the *quietest* of the four reveals — a tea in São Carlos, an old man unlocking a drawer. It pays off Iris's preparation.
- **IV (Pamir)** is the destination. Going to it closes the campaign-level Pamir arc — the cave, the wall inscriptions, the second sphere.

The set is built so that no two fragments offer the same kind of session. Vienna is institutional infiltration. Todd is intimate. Pamir is the journey.

# Open at the table

- **The "right name" Todd is waiting on.** Pick from Bernard's player canon; should be something only Silvertongue family would know.
- **Klaus's posture toward fragment II.** Does he move it to the Vienna private vault when he hears the PCs survived, or leave it on display assuming nobody knows?
- **The fate of the two lost Vienna-shipped pieces** — destroyed in transit, customs-stolen, or sitting in a third archive nobody has named yet. Reserved as a later hook if needed.
- **How fragments physically join.** Sketch reconstructions can be assembled from photographs (Bernard + Todd + Vienna photo) before any pieces meet; the physical assembly is a final-act ritual moment in the cave with IV.
