# Tablet HF-A-1 — the four fragments

The Sumerian tablet the PCs took from the Building B Main Library safe is **HF-A-1**, one of five tablets in the historical set Silvertongue worked on. HF-A-1 itself broke into four irregular pieces in antiquity — Wittenbach and his young field partner Silvertongue found it already in pieces in a cave alcove in 1885 and shipped them out under separate cover. They have been apart ever since.

The party carries one piece. The other three sit in three different places, each tied to a different arc.

---

## Fragment I — Bottom-left (the party)

Already in play. Bernard carries it. Contains the western arm of the hexagonal map, the Hindu Kush edge, and a partial inscription line. Was in the Halder collection because Wittenbach's notebooks reached the Esterhazy archive in 1903 and Marian Esterhazy quietly traded a single piece to Halder's grandfather in the early 1960s as part of the founding gesture.

---

## Fragment II — Vienna (Esterhazy archive)

**Where:** in the Esterhazy library in Vienna, shelved beside Wittenbach's original 1880s field notebooks. Has been there since 1903. Klaus knows it exists. The Committee has never read it as load-bearing — to them it has always been an antiquarian footnote next to Wittenbach's far more useful oral-tradition notes.

**What's on it:** the centre of the hexagonal map. The target marker. The sexagesimal numerals.

**Why it matters:** without the centre piece, Bernard's bottom-left fragment is only an edge — directional, not located. Vienna closes the map.

### Clues the PCs can find pointing at Vienna

These are arranged from easy to hard, so the table can layer them depending on how much the players are pulling.

1. **Mansion archive (when they reach it).** A 1962 typed inventory of *Esterhazy library, on-loan and on-deposit Halder-relevant material,* compiled by Halder's grandfather, lists *"Pamir tablet fragments II–IV (Wittenbach 1885), Esterhazy archive Vienna, on-deposit, irrevocable."* The word *irrevocable* is underlined in pencil — Halder's hand. Beside it, a margin note: *"Marian will not release. M.E. holds remaining three."*

2. **Silvertongue's field log** (Mansion archive copy, or the original in the family archive at the old Cambridge house). Silvertongue was present in the alcove that morning and wrote up the discovery himself. The entry reads, in his careful Cambridge hand: *"Four pieces, broken anciently, scattered about the alcove. Sketched in situ. Three loose on the floor and taken; the fourth wedged too deep in the binding-stone to free without breaking the inscription, sketched and left. Of the three: two shipped to V. via the Russian post; one retained for the drawing."* The *one retained* became fragment I; the *two shipped to V.* became fragments II and III. The *one left in the wall* became fragment IV.

3. **Edmund's Foundation records.** A 1972 visit photograph — Halder's father, J. Halder II, in his thirties, on a courtesy visit to the Esterhazy library. Behind him, on a side table, a small clay piece in a felt-lined display tray. **Only one piece visible.** If the PCs run an image search through Edmund's research assistant, the photograph surfaces in a Foundation Board annual report from 1973.

4. **Yates knows but will not volunteer.** Yates accessed the Silvertongue family archive in 2014; he read the same alcove field log. If the PCs reach Yates and ask plainly, he will confirm Vienna holds at least one — but he will also note something Halder's grandfather never wrote down: *"The Esterhazy register lists three. Only one has ever been seen on display. The other two, if they exist, are in the private vault."* This is the second-order beat — Klaus may know more than the public inventory suggests.

### Status of the two shipped fragments

Of the two pieces Wittenbach shipped to Vienna, only one (fragment II) arrived intact and entered the Esterhazy archive in 1903. The other (fragment III) never reached Vienna — Russian-post damage, customs intercept, a lost crate; the table can pick the cause when it matters. III eventually surfaced in Silvertongue's hands in Cambridge, by whatever route the table chooses to specify, and stayed in the family.

**Functionally all four fragments are live: I (party), II (Vienna), III (Todd), IV (Pamir).** Fragment I's edge + II's centre + III's inscription + IV's site reading assemble the full message.

## Fragment III — Todd has it (secret)

**Where:** in Prof. Aaron Todd's office at USP São Carlos, in a locked drawer under correspondence files. Wrapped in cotton inside a small wooden cigar box. He has had it since 1989.

**How he got it:** the tea visit in 1989 — already in canon (STORY.md §1998–2024 / the Todd thread). Hartwell brought Todd to the old Cambridge house one afternoon as a postgrad. Bernard's maternal grandfather — Silvertongue's son, then in his eighties and the family's quiet steward of the Pamir work — made a decision he had been weighing for years: he handed Todd a small wrapped object and asked him to *"keep it until someone in my family asks for it by the right name."* Todd does not know what the right name is. The grandfather did not tell him. Hartwell may have known but is dead.

Todd kept the piece for thirty-five years without knowing exactly what it was. He suspected; he did not press. In late 2024, when Iris pre-briefed him through Hartwell's widow as an intermediary, Iris confirmed what the piece was and asked him to hold it until Bernard came for it. **Todd has been waiting for Bernard's call since.**

**What's on it:** the right side of the hexagonal map and the bulk of the proto-cuneiform inscription — the readable text. Without this, the map is a map without instructions.

**The reveal beat.** When Bernard contacts Todd through any normal channel and identifies himself as Silvertongue's great-grandson, Todd asks one question — something only the family would know (open at the table; pick from Bernard's existing player canon). On the right answer, Todd opens the drawer. This is the moment Iris designed for; it is not a clue chain, it is a recognition scene.

### Optional foreshadowing breadcrumbs (use sparingly)

- **In the Silvertongue family archive** (the small library at the old Cambridge house, reachable through the maternal-line relative who currently holds it): a handwritten note dated November 1989 in Bernard's grandfather's hand reading *"the small piece I let A. carry south."* "A." is Aaron Todd. Bernard can decode this if he reads the grandfather's correspondence carefully.
- **Hartwell's widow** can be drawn out to mention an old letter from Bernard's grandfather to her husband from late 1989 that included a phrase about *"a piece in safe hands across the equator."* Hartwell kept it in a private drawer at the Cambridge MA house; the widow has the key but never opened it.
- **Iris's prepared materials** (the dossier they may still be carrying or that Casetti may surface): a single line, *"Todd holds III. Identify yourself. He will know."*

None of the above is required. Bernard contacting Todd is the trigger; the breadcrumbs only exist for tables where Bernard's player would rather have earned the reveal.

## Fragment IV — The Pamir site

**Where:** at the Pamir corridor itself, near the head of the Fedchenko glacier, in the cave canon already places there (STORY.md §503 — the cave with the original tribe's wall inscriptions and the second sealed sphere). The fragment was never removed from the site. Wittenbach saw four pieces in the alcove and took only three out; the fourth was too deep into the wall, embedded in old binding-stone, and he could not pry it free without destroying the inscription around it. He left it. It is still there.

**What's on it:** the southern arm of the hexagonal map and the closing line of the inscription — the line that names what the other three pieces together direct the reader to find. Silvertongue's polished translation closes with this line. Without IV in hand the reader can decode the *location* of the cave but not the *meaning* of what the cave holds.

**Clues:** none needed. The map itself, once assembled from I + II + III, points the party to the Pamir. They go because the map tells them to go. The fourth fragment is the reward for arriving, not a separate hunt.

# Why this distribution works

- **I (party)** is what they hold. The hook.
- **II (Vienna)** forces a Committee-adjacent leg. Klaus's town, his archive, his patronage relationships. The party has to either steal it, lean on Edmund's Foundation channels for a legitimate request, or use Yates's old route. Each opens different threads.
- **III (Todd)** rewards the relationship Bernard already has. It is the *quietest* of the four reveals — a tea in São Carlos, an old man unlocking a drawer. It pays off Iris's preparation.
- **IV (Pamir)** is the destination. Going to it closes the campaign-level Pamir arc — the cave, the wall inscriptions, the second sphere.

The set is built so that no two fragments offer the same kind of session. Vienna is institutional infiltration. Todd is intimate. Pamir is the journey.

---

# The Pamir cave

The cave is not sealed. It is a natural limestone chamber with a low entrance the PCs can walk into. The map points them there; the journey costs them; the entry does not.

Ten thousand years ago, the chase between the PCs and the Listener climaxed at or just inside the cave. The tablet broke in the chaos, scattering four pieces around the alcove. Three settled loose on the floor (becoming I, II, III over the millennia, via Wittenbach in 1885). The fourth wedged deep into a band of binding-stone in the back wall and stayed there — Wittenbach saw it in 1885, could not pry it free without destroying the inscription around it, and left it. It has not moved since. The PCs (post-loop) can chip it out, transcribe it in situ, or leave it.

There is no slab, no recess, no puzzle key. The four pieces together complete the inscription's *meaning*; they do not open any door.
