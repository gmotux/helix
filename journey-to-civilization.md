# Journey to Civilisation

The tunnel under the campus to the warehouse on Boston Harbor. Designed for table use, not narration prose. Encounters concrete, rooms specific, contents enumerable when the players ask.

---

## Geography and distance

The freight-rail tunnel runs **southwest from JOINP (northeast of Boston) toward the inner harbour, ~48 km.** It is not on any public infrastructure map. Built by the Halder Foundation in 1963 on top of pre-1900 industrial works — a long-disused Saugus-Lynn iron-foundry rail spur — and quietly extended in the 70s to reach a Halder-owned warehouse on the Mystic River side of the inner harbour. (Mystic River = a real waterway forming Boston's northern harbour edge, between Charlestown and Chelsea. Working-class industrial waterfront. Container yards, scrap-metal yards, fuel depots, small bulk-cargo brokers.)

**Two rail lines run in parallel** for the full length of the tunnel — a north line and a south line, each with its own track and contact rail. Standard freight gauge. The maintenance train the PCs found below Building B was on the south line; the one they will find here is on the north line.

### Walking pace

The PCs are exhausted, low on resources, carrying gear. Walking pace through a damaged tunnel with debris, dim light, breaks, and obstacles: **~3 km/h.** Total time end-to-end: **~16 hours.** They will spend a full night underground. They will need to rest at least once.

---

## Tunnel state

Three structural segments:

1. **Campus end (first 4 km).** Compromised by the strike. Ceiling cracks, rock falls, dripping. The broken maintenance train they came from is here. Air heavy with concrete dust. No mist remaining — the blast overpressure displaced it back into the campus zone.

2. **Middle stretch (next ~38 km).** Intact but never properly maintained. The tunnel was a sealed asset; the people who used it for cargo did not need it to look nice. 1960s electrical conduits, dead lights, exposed pipework, standing water at low points. Pre-1900 brick-arch construction alternating with mid-century concrete repair patches. Atmospheric, dark, the PCs' lights are the only light. **This is where the two scripted encounters happen and where the rest point is.**

3. **Boston end (last 6 km).** Better-maintained because of recent cargo traffic. Functional drainage. Newer rail. Mid-stretch power still works at intermittent fixtures. Approaches the warehouse via a sub-basement freight platform.

# The beats

## Beat 1 — the rest point

About **14 km in**, the tunnel passes through a small foreman's alcove from the 1960s — a single steel door set into the brick on the south wall, leading to a square room about three metres on a side. Heavy iron door that closes from inside, small high vent for air, room enough for four people to lie down on the concrete floor. The room has a working hand-pump for water (rusty for the first few seconds, then clear) and a 1960s electric outlet that is live (whoever maintained the tunnel's intermittent power left this circuit alive).

**This is the safe rest point.** Defensible — only one approach, single door. **The PCs can short-rest or long-rest here.** Recommended at the table: a long rest here before the rat fight at Beat 2. Eight hours costs them time and the world above shifts (Saturday morning rolls around, the news cycle has moved), but they will need it.

There is nothing else of value in the alcove. A 1970s thermos on a shelf, empty. A wall calendar from 1988 with a bikini-model on the cover, faded. A pencilled tally on the door frame counting something nobody remembers.

## Beat 2 — the breeding chamber

About **22 km in**, the tunnel widens for a section where the 1900-era iron foundry used to have a transfer pit. A side chamber, vaulted brick, used in the Kallisto era as a maintenance dead-end for switching freight cars off the line.

A pack of rats lived here for a hundred years. **A week ago, mist that had been seeping down from the Building B basement through the tunnel air system reached this far and pooled in the low point.** The pool dissipated forty hours ago. Whatever rats were in the chamber when it was active are not rats anymore.

### Encounter — the brood

## Beat 4 — the mist pools

Three low points in the rest of the middle stretch. Knee-deep slow mist pooled and stagnant, lethal to helix-negatives, harmless to the PCs.

At the **second pool**, the mist responds to the Asterweiss sphere when it passes overhead — eddies around it like air around a magnet. **First hint that the sphere is not inert and that the flower inside is calling to its field.** No mechanical effect; the PCs notice. Bernard, if he's paying attention, will feel a half-second of harmonic resonance from his own hum at 528 Hz against the sphere; this is the singer's gene answering the flower's.

No encounter at the mist pools. Atmosphere only. The PCs walk through the mist with no harm and notice it parting for them.

## Beat 5 — the hatch

At ~46 km, end of the middle stretch, the tunnel narrows and runs into a heavy steel security hatch. Painted Beacon Marine Cargo livery on the inside face — anyone using this tunnel was supposed to come from the warehouse side. From the PCs' side: a featureless steel wall set into the concrete, a small reader panel, and a mechanical combination lock above the panel.

**The reader panel** is a biometric scanner — palm-and-fingertip. It is configured to authorise Kallisto staff with current biometric records. **The PCs' helix-positive expression confuses it.** Three failed scans put the scanner into a *manual override required* state; this is what the PCs will trip into.

**The combination lock** is a six-digit dial. The combination is **030461** (from Park's notebook scrawl in Beat 1). Entering the combination releases the hatch.

If the PCs entered without the notebook: the lock cannot be brute-forced in any reasonable time, but Bernard with a careful tinkers'-tools check (DC 18 Intelligence) can hear the tumblers from the hum the dial makes against his ears, given his expression. Single attempt. If they fail and don't find the notebook, they're stuck — the tunnel does not have another exit. (Hand them the notebook if they get to this point without it.)

Past the hatch: a short tiled corridor with overhead lighting (still working), a security camera (its recording loop has been on a four-hour cycle since the warehouse went unmanned — the PCs will be on tape but nobody is reviewing), and a freight elevator. The elevator works on a separate hatch keycard which Park has on his lanyard. Press the only button. Up.

# The warehouse

## Establishing context

**Beacon Marine Cargo** is the front company. Officially: a small marine-freight brokerage doing specialised refrigerated bulk shipments out of Boston Harbor. Real function: Halder Foundation sample transfer to and from the Antarctic base via chartered vessels. **Six full-time staff:** a manager, two warehousemen, one office administrator, one forklift operator, one security/dock contact. All on paid leave since the morning of April 14, called by an unidentified Foundation number that said simply "you'll be back at work when we call you back."

The warehouse sits at **Pier 3, Mystic Wharf Industrial Park, Chelsea, Massachusetts** — the working industrial waterfront on the north side of Boston Harbor, fifteen minutes from downtown Boston by car. Surrounded by container yards, a fuel depot, a sand-and-gravel terminal, and a 24-hour Dunkin' Donuts two blocks inland.

**Timing on surface.** The PCs reach the warehouse at roughly **14:00 Saturday April 18** — about T+24 hours after the strike (16 hours' walk + 8 hours' long rest at Beat 1). Saturday afternoon on a working industrial dock with most of the workforce off. The sand-and-gravel terminal two berths down is operating with a skeleton weekend crew (three guys, a forklift, indifferent to anything that isn't loaded into their hopper). Pleasure-craft river traffic up the Mystic is normal. Distant military air traffic visible over the harbour. Edmund Halder is on every cable channel doing his first televised press conference *(see `misinformation.md`, T+20–T+24 window).*

## How the PCs identify the owners

The PCs do their own work. A reverse-lookup of any of these numbers on the internet returns ownership data — Casetti and Todd are both academics with public university pages, ResearchGate profiles, and published work.

The PCs then *piece together* why Iris pointed at these people:

- **Casetti's 1991 paper** *"Non-destructive characterisation of historic crystalline specimens"* is in JSTOR. Whoever reads it realises she has examined an artefact whose described properties match the Asterweiss sphere the PCs are carrying. She is the only outside specialist on record who has ever touched it.
- **Todd's 1998 Kallisto-funded paper** *"Cognitive resonance and harmonic perception in atypical subjects"* is also in JSTOR. Whoever reads it sees that he was a Kallisto contractor in the 1990s and that his work matches Bernard's harmonic expression closely. **Bernard recognises the name** — Todd is his PhD supervisor at USP São Carlos. Bernard had no idea Todd had ever worked for Kallisto.

That second realisation is the load-bearing moment: Bernard has been Todd's student for years, and Todd has been doing classified work adjacent to the helix programme for longer than Bernard has been alive. Bernard now has to decide whether to trust him.

## How identity is verified at the table

Iris pre-briefed both contacts in 2024 to expect a call. Neither contact uses a magic password. Both verify by asking questions the legitimate callers can answer from what they currently have or can currently do:

- **Casetti** asks the caller to describe the sphere — its surface texture, where the small chip is on the equator, the unusual asymmetry in its acoustic ring. The PCs are holding the sphere; they look at it and answer. Anyone else can't.
- **Todd** asks for **Bernard, by name**, and asks Bernard to hum into the phone for ten seconds. Bernard hums his characteristic 528 Hz. Todd has thirteen years of CRT cassette recordings Patel sent him; he runs the phone audio against them in real time. Identity confirmed by acoustic fingerprint. Anyone else fails.

- **A safe USP lab** for analysing anything the PCs bring (vials, residue samples, sphere data after Casetti).
- **An academic-network back-channel** — Todd can reach Edmund Halder discreetly without going through any US-monitored line.
- **His own suspicions.** Todd has been quietly noticing for twenty years that the cassettes Patel sends him contain harmonic signatures from subjects who do not show up in any public research literature. He has been keeping a private journal. He will share it with the PCs.

**What he doesn't know:** what the gene actually is, that S-0 exists, that Bernard's mother carried the gene. He suspects Bernard is unusual; he doesn't know how.

**Why two numbers.** Cellphone is private but more exposed to metadata-pattern analysis; office line routes through the USP switchboard and is harder to attribute to a specific caller. The cell is for direct/urgent contact, the office line for first-contact discretion. Todd will steer the PCs on which to use.

**Voice cue:** Quiet, slightly Brazilian-inflected English. After Bernard's hum confirms: *"Bernard. Tell me where you are. Tell me what you have."*

## DM notes on the contacts

- Both contacts are real, reachable, and willing. Neither will betray the PCs. Neither knows enough to be dangerous if interrogated.
- Neither can travel to the US in the short term. Casetti will not leave Naples; Todd's Brazilian passport and academic visibility make a US trip an unacceptable risk. **The PCs travel to them when the time is right.**
- **Casetti is the Asterweiss-cracking play.** The mutation track makes it urgent eventually.
- **Todd is the deeper play.** He is a six-node connector to Silvertongue, Patel, Yates, Edmund Halder, Iris, and Bernard himself. Once the PCs reach him, they have a hub for almost every thread the campaign opens after the cabin.
